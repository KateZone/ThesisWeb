3.1.2 Energy Governance and Economic Development

Affected by the overall economic downturn, the total energy consumption of large-scale industries in Shenyang has been decreasing continuously since 2012. The total energy consumption in 2015 was only 74.7% of that in 2011, and the energy consumption per unit GDP also decreased year over year. As such, it’s clear that Shenyang's energy consumption is affected by GDP.

Shenyang’s GDP in 2019 was 647.03 billion yuan, an increase of 4.2% over the previous year when adjusted for inflation. The added value of industries accounted for 4.4% of GDP; the secondary industry 33.7%, and the tertiary industry 61.9% (Shenyang Statistic Bureau, 2020).

In 2020, the city's GDP reached 657.16 billion yuan, with a year-on-year growth of 0.8% after adjusting for inflation. By industry, the added value of the primary industry was 30.36 billion yuan, up 2.9% year on year; the secondary industry was 216.04 billion yuan, up 2.9%; and the tertiary industry was 410.76 billion yuan, down 0.6% (Shenyang Statistic Bureau, 2021).

Therefore, the relationship between economic growth and energy consumption in Shenyang is clear. The economy of Shenyang is a non-energy-dependent economy, which corresponds to its industrial structure as a proportion of GDP.

The emerging market economy has a serious impact on entities that flourished during the planned economy. In the planned economy era, energy supply was unidirectional, with low and stable prices. During the dual-track system, state-owned enterprises could enjoy energy subsidies. However, after these subsidies were canceled, energy types and prices were set by supply and demand.

More than half of the users now pay individually for heating, leading to a shift from centralized to decentralized heating. Shenyang has a coal-dependent energy consumption structure, with coal accounting for about 60% of energy use. Direct combustion of raw coal, with 37% of coal usage having sulfur content over 1%, contributes heavily to CO2 emissions and environmental issues.

Many cities in China, like Shenyang, face domestic pressure to reduce carbon emissions. Achieving peak carbon emissions is crucial for sustainable economic growth, requiring a decoupling of CO2 emissions from economic growth.

Kaya’s Equation

According to Kaya’s famous equation (Kaya, 1990):

CO2 Emission = Population × GDP/Population × Energy Consumption/GDP × CO2 Emission/Energy Consumption

Without major adjustments in energy sector and efficiency, there will be a linear relationship between energy consumption and CO2 emissions, with carbon intensity constant. Therefore, CO2 Emission/Energy Consumption = E, where E = 2.45.

Shenyang has made some progress in reducing CO2 emissions per capita. However, it is still dependent on high-pollution industries for economic growth, with local governments prioritizing these sectors to meet GDP targets.
